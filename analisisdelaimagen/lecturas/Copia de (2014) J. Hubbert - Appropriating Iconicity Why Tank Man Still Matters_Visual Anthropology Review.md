# **Appropriating Iconicity: Why Tank Man Still Matters**

JENNIFER HUBBERT

*This article explores the reappropriation of iconic photographs, examining what happens when the iconic "Tank Man" image is modified and repurposed to new political ends. It argues that such reappropriations push viewers to read against the grain of ontological security because the ubiquity of the originals motivates the questioning of taken-for-granted meaning in new contexts. In this case, the 21st-century reappropriated Tank Man images speak less about the absence of political liberalism and democracy in China that were encapsulated in the original Tank Man image and more about their absence in the contemporary United States. At the same time, they also reflect changing global hierarchies in which the United States no longer automatically occupies a position of power relative to China. As such, reappropriations of iconic photographs have the potential to mobilize collective memory and political imaginations to new ends.* [appropriation, China, icon, photography, Tank Man, Tiananmen]

# **Introduction**

**I** n the spring of 1989, following a decade of post-Mao economic and social reforms, the world witnessed the spectacle of mass demonstrations in Beijing's enormous Tiananmen Square. Over a period of six weeks, as many as a million citizens demonstrated to call upon the Chinese government to abolish official corruption, expand the human rights of its citizens, and move toward a more democratic society. On June 4, after the government's declaration of martial law had failed to clear the demonstrators, armed soldiers and armored vehicles swept through the square and surrounding areas, killing what is estimated to be hundreds of people and effectively putting an end to the protest movement.

The following day, a lone man, holding a plastic shopping bag in each hand, stepped directly in front of an approaching column of armored tanks. The lead tank worked to circumvent the man, but at each stage was blocked as the protestor repeatedly repositioned himself to impede its forward progress. Once the column of tanks surrendered its attempts to outmaneuver the pedestrian, the man climbed atop the nearest tank and engaged in a conversation with the driver who emerged from within. Shortly thereafter, the protestor was whisked away by two anonymous bystanders. Although numerous media sources have speculated about the young man's identity, none have been able to confirm who he was or what happened to him following the incident.

Sometimes known as the "Unknown Rebel," the young protestor is most commonly referred to as "Tank Man." A photograph of Tank Man confronting the hulking machines appeared on the front page of *the New York Times* the following day, and on the cover of *Time* magazine shortly thereafter, and emerged as one of the most iconic photographs of the 20th century. As correspondent Pico Iyer notes, Tank Man "was seen in his moment of self-transcendence by more people than ever laid eyes on Winston Churchill, Albert Einstein and James Joyce combined" (Iyer 1998). In recognition, *Life Magazine* later immortalized the image of Tank Man as one of the "100 photos that changed the world" (Sullivan 2003).

This article examines what happens when iconic photographs such as that of Tank Man are repurposed, modified, and appropriated to new political ends. While other scholars have explored Tank Man's *in situ* meaning and performative effect,1 in this article I analyze more recent appropriations of the Tank Man icon in Western political cartoons, popular Internet memes, and political posters, and dissect their significance in the contemporary era. These appropriations borrow the original structural format of the photograph—a lone figure confronting a column of tanks—but modify its content and conceptual implications, exploding what Barthes (1981) has termed the madness of ideology by making it present. As Arjun Appadurai (1986) traces the variable and complex meaning of commodities as they move through different experiential and historical regimes, this article evaluates the conflicting temporal and spatial trajectories through which meaning is inscribed upon the Tank Man image, and appeals to viewers as an icon of the relative value and global position of China and the West.

As I will argue, the reappropriated 21st-century Tank Man images analyzed here speak less about the absence of political liberalism and democracy in China (encapsulated in the original Tank Man image) than they do about their absence in the contemporary United States. At the same time, they also reflect changing global hierarchies in which the United States no longer automatically occupies a position of power relative to China. Ultimately, this analysis suggests that the performance of an icon has power not only because of its ability to capture so meaningfully the significance and value of its original context, but also because the takenfor-granted meaning that contributes to its ubiquity motivates questioning in new contexts, pushing viewers to read against the grain of perceptions of representational stability.

# **Anthropology and Photography: Situating Tank Man as Icon and Ideology**

Photography, in its most essential form, is perceived as standard evidence of historical events, as objective "data" (Chiozzi 1989) much like a bar graph or a set of anthropological fieldnotes.2 When a photo becomes iconic, however—when the image achieves "wholly exceptional levels of widespread recognizability" (Kemp 2012:3)—it enters the realm of the goes-without-saying, bearing "witness to something that exceeds words" (Hariman and Lucaites 2007:1). Such icons have, as Stanley Tambiah (1981) has noted, a performative effect. That is, we do not merely view iconic photographs, but also learn from them, including how to be certain kinds of subjects, what to hope for, and how to assess value.3 As others have described, the apparent artlessness and inevitability of the ascribed meaning and values of icons shape our narrative responses to them through their reinscription of dominant ways of seeing the world.4

As a presumed evidentiary form of data, the Tank Man image quickly emerged as the dominant symbolic representation of the events of Tiananmen, 1989. *Time* magazine explained that "the brief encounter between the man and the tank captured an epochal event," and David Perlmutter labeled the image the "supericon of Tiananmen" (1998:66). But although the photograph of Tank Man quickly came to stand in for the 1989 protest movement, dominating newspaper front pages, magazine covers, and television airwaves, the image was never solely about the event itself. The "tiny young man," as Slavoj Žižek has called the Tank Man figure, also offered the West a moment of "transparent clarity" about China (Žižek 2002:45–46). Indeed, as Michael Dutton notes, the pictured man confronting machine

is the streetscene that constitutes the mind's-flash image of the Western world. . . . The representation is so powerful that it demolishes all other understandings. This streetscene, this time and this event, have come to constitute the compass point for *virtually all Western journeys into the interior of the contemporary political and cultural life of China*. [Dutton 1998:17, emphasis added].

As such, Tank Man exists not only as an iconic embodiment of a protest movement but also of the Chinese nation itself, a representation of what some have argued was the "most emblematic event of Post-Mao China" from the perspective of the Western world (Vukovich 2012:130).

While Tank Man as a public icon conflated representation with event and embodied the "local" of China, it was never a purely "local" image. Although the Tank Man photo and other iconic photographs are understood as a form of evidence, they are also used to mark differences that represent hierarchies of value. Such visual regimes of veracity—or what Sabra Thorner, drawing from Martin Jay, in her study of photographs of Aboriginal Australians, calls "scopic regimes"—tend to racialize and dispossess their photographic objects (Thorner 2008:66), establishing moral hierarchies and ideologies of identification.5 What consequently made the Tank Man photo so powerful as an iconic moment of "truth" was the apparent obviousness of what it taught us, not only about the events of 1989, but as the photo circulated throughout the world, also about the "horrors of Communist China" (Schaffer and Smith 2004:204).6 This seemed to confirm, as we will explore below, that heroism is located in the individual, and it reassured the American viewer of the United States' position as the moral leader of the "free world" and foremost advocate of democracy and human rights.

Anthropology has long recognized the problematic nature of the relationship between evidence and photography, focusing attention both upon the photograph as a constructed rather than an evidentiary moment and upon its function as a mechanism for the enactment of power.7 Anthropology's problematization of the photograph represents a methodological shift away from perceptions of fixed meaning and toward an examination of the social biography of the photo, or the production of relations through which meaning, power, and history are articulated (Edwards 2005), thereby emphasizing what Christopher Pinney has called the "perpetual principal of dissatisfaction" with the assessment of visual meaning (Pinney 2012:11). Yet for the Tank Man image to become and remain iconic, the viewer must suspend dissatisfaction and disregard the social specificity of the object. The Tank Man protestor's role as a protagonist of history, that is, depends upon the perpetual concealment of the political ideologies that reinforce the photograph's obviousness and upon ignorance of what occurred beyond the frame.

In the case of the Tank Man icon, this first requires understanding the Chinese state as a monolithic, reified entity, ignoring the voices of multiple political and military officials who expressed opposition to martial law. The effect of such framing is, as Robert Hariman and John Lucaites argue about iconic images, to see Tank Man "like a state," a representation of the liberal, modernist project that "leaves the public culture with diminished resources for democratic action" (2007:23). What Barthes argues for the text surrounding the photo is true for the photo itself: it is a "costly" and "repressive" fragment that excludes alternative messages (Barthes 1980:273–276). It is also through its fragmentary nature that the Tank Man icon is invested with and made to perpetuate morality and ideology. The photo as commonly cropped and available to us largely in still rather than video form deletes all traces of mass involvement. It contains no sounds, no dialogue between the protestor and the tank driver that might allow the viewer to see the driver as a human making a moral or political decision of his own, no voices of the million-odd others who gathered in the square that might frame effective protest as a function of the masses rather than the individual.

Second, for the Tank Man photograph to retain its apparent semiotic certainty, it needs to be understood not merely as a representation of a reified state but of a specifically "communist" state. The image, as many have noted, was understood to signify the "horrors of Communist China" (Schaffer and Smith 2004:204) and the "the brutality and inhumanity of the CCP regime" (Brady 2008:160). The *Washington Post*'s Tank Man feature declared, "Here is the vaunted evil of communism in living color" (Cohen 1989), and *Time* magazine's June 19, 1989 cover featured the Tank Man photo positioned over a bold caption that read, "Revolt against Communism." Indeed, through the textual commentary, communism emerges as a code for totalitarianism, understood in this case as synonymous with the Chinese state.

Lastly, the Tank Man image reminded the viewer not only about the "inherent" nature of communism, but also that the ideal form of state–society relations is one in which the individual citizen is the source of legitimacy and the locus of power. On June 6, 1989, for example, *the New York Times* centered the Tank Man photograph on its front page, the title of the accompanying article asserting, "Crackdown in Beijing; One Man Can Make a Difference," and beginning with the deceptively simple words, "It all started with a man in a white shirt." In the absence of explicit analysis, the article ended with then-President Bush's reflections on the protestor, in which "a single student standing in front of a tank" becomes "the forces of democracy [that] are going to overcome these unfortunate events in Tiananmen Square." Through this ideological construction, the image in the photograph emerges as the ideal articulation of political practice that, as Dutton suggests, "fulfilled western yearnings for an individual ethics of heroism" (Dutton 1998:18), a guide for the desires of the private citizen who could aspire to what the West had already achieved (Ghosh 2011:46). In the process, the Tank Man icon reassured the United States of its position as leader of the free world (Ghosh 2011:48) and of the inherent, nonideological value of its political system and political practices in which the individual has the rightful and tangible capacity to act, reinforcing perceptions about the presumed superiority of civil society in the West.

The power of photos to evoke emotion and common values—the obviousness bestowed by iconicity—is also a product of the certainties of particular historical eras. Their performative impact, in other words, is dependent upon the extant regimes of value that contextualize the images. Desire is key to this experience. As Brian Larkin (2013) explains of the circulation of Hindi film in Nigeria, what is discursively obvious is what we desire.8 Shortly following Tank Man's post-Tiananmen entrance into the Western public sphere, China became an international pariah, condemned for its violence against citizens and seeming political intransigence. Businesses that had eagerly entered China just years before pulled out of the country in droves, and governments around the world cut off high-level contacts. Most of China's Eastern Bloc allies were abandoning their communist experiments, and the Berlin Wall, perhaps the most potent visual symbol of the Cold War, had literally been torn down. The Cold War battle had seemingly been "won," and its chief victor, the United States, occupied a vaunted economic and political position in the global hierarchy of nation states. The United States, in its presumed role as the champion of freedom and democracy, thus served as the implicit ideological foil for the Chinese tanks in the Tank Man image.

Two and a half decades later, the iconic photo of Tank Man continues to appear in the U.S. public sphere as a cipher for human rights and as an idealized representation of political practice that configures Westernstyle democracy as the antithesis of communist oppression.9 Yet the intervening years have also witnessed enough of a shift in global hierarchies of power and possibilities for democratic participation to unsettle some of the earlier sureties of semiotic interpretation. In the grip of the Great Recession and the continuing exodus of jobs to China and other low-wage nations, the economy of the United States and other Western powers appeared precariously perched even as China became the world's biggest exporter and second-largest economy. As China's international reputation and economic and political relations with other nations around the globe improved, those of the United States waned in light of its unpopular post-9/11 military interventions in the Middle East and increasing surveillance at home.

# **Tank Man in the Contemporary Imaginary: Appropriating Iconicity**

It is at this point in global history that the appropriated Tank Man icon began to emerge in forms that highlighted a moment of "dissatisfaction" and gave credence to the photo as a social object. These new images, often humorous and sometimes disturbing, clearly alluded to the original but modified its potential meaning, this time offering a critique of rather than a paean to democratic and human rights practices in the West.10 In myriad sources, including popular Internet sites, mainstream newspapers, and magazines, some images positioned a lone protestor in front of Chinese tanks, including Homer Simpson's sister-inlaw and President Barack Obama, while others replaced the tanks with long lines of Hummers, cargo trucks filled with toxic products, grocery carts, or rubber ducks. The critical targets of these appropriations were similarly varied, ranging from inflation and globalization to environmental degradation and partisan politics. The thematic similarity of these images—despite their dramatic visual difference—raises powerful questions about the portability of iconicity. Because the image has become so ubiquitous and its meaning generally so taken for granted, its appropriation seems so evidently matter-out-of-place as to merit reflection upon that matter itself. Although as Bishnupriya Ghosh has argued, such appropriations do not necessarily revise the legacy of the Tank Man image (2011:50), this contemporary repurposing reflects changing contemporary socioeconomic and political contexts that have led artists and commentators to challenge earlier assumptions about political practice and global hierarchies of power. This historiographic interrogation intervenes in taken-for-granted readings of political practice, "speaking disorder to order" (in the felicitous phrase of Jennifer Jackson [2013:112]) to question the processes and outcomes of iconization in a new global context.11

For the purposes of this article, I examine four of these appropriations that seem to speak most directly to dominant Western interpretations of the Tank Man image as an icon of human rights, democracy, and global hierarchies of power. The first two are political cartoons published on the occasion of high-level meetings between members of the Obama administration and Chinese officials during high-profile visits to China. At first glance, these two images offer commentary about the global repositioning of China and the United States that is clearly intended to mock the United States for surrendering to Chinese state power and sacrificing its historical advocacy of human rights. As such, the images suggest new forms of relatedness, not only between viewer and iconic certainty (De Lorenzo 2006), but also between the nations invoked both explicitly and implicitly in the original icon. In contrast, the second two images, one an Internet meme and the other a political poster, address the domestic context of the Occupy Wall Street movement. In these two images, although the original Tank Man image remains an obvious referent, in some ways China itself becomes largely irrelevant to their critique of diminishing democratic and human rights protections within the United States itself. The de-making and remaking of these images offer a process of "retrogressive remediation" (Spyer and Steedly 2013:22–23) that pushes the viewer to recognize and denaturalize the processes of framing through which the image is given meaning and in the process to question its ontological certainty. The Tank Man icon, visually re-presented in these appropriations, is no longer a verification of the ideological certainties of the original but a site of contestation.

### *Global Hierarchies of Power*

The first image I examine features the figure of Chinese civil rights activist Chen Guangcheng, whom *Time* magazine included in its 2006 list of the world's 100 most influential individuals. Blind and self-educated, Chen has suffered beatings and served time in prison for his legal advocacy on behalf of women and impoverished rural citizens in China. In 2012, days before Secretary of State Hillary Clinton was to visit China for an economic summit, Chen escaped from house arrest and fled to the U.S. Embassy in Beijing. The *New York Times* described the escape as a "headache" for the Obama administration, which was determined to "improve its fraught relationship with Beijing," but also aware that Chen's safety could not be guaranteed outside the embassy's walls (Jacobs and Ansfield 2012). While Chen was negotiating with Chinese officials for his freedom, Clinton was negotiating with the Chinese government over trade deficits and currency devaluation. Although the U.S. State Department denied his account, in CNN and Associated Press interviews, Chen maintained that American officials pressured him to leave the embassy, one assumes in order to maintain cordial relations with their diplomatic hosts.12 Chen ultimately left the embassy and was taken to a local hospital for treatment of the broken ankle he suffered during his escape, where he was reunited with his family and eventually granted an exit visa for the United States. In her opening address at the U.S.–China Strategic and Economic Dialogue in Beijing the day after Chen left the embassy, Clinton raised the issue of human rights, arguing, "all governments do have to answer to citizens' aspirations for dignity and the rule of law" (Clinton 2012), but never mentioned Chen specifically. Several days following Chen's exit from the embassy and Clinton's speech in Beijing, political cartoonist Gary Varvel published a cartoon featuring the Chen incident that directly mimicked the layout of the original Tank Man photograph: a lone figure confronting a column of four menacing tanks (Varvel 2012). Yet, in this new image, Varvel replaced the anonymous protestor with activist Chen, seated in a wheelchair, whose arm reaches into the air as if attempting to halt the forward advance of the machines. In this appropriation, the protestor is no longer silent and the viewer "hears" him shouting, "Hillary, I fear for my safety. . .Hillary?. . .Hillary?!" Chen's increasingly desperate cry for support suggests that the United States has abandoned its support of microlevel human rights in favor of a smoother macrolevel relationship at the level of the state. In contrast to the State Department's later description of the summit between Clinton and Chinese officials as affirming their "commitment to continuing. . .dialogue. . .on human rights on the basis of equality and mutual respect" and "acknowledging our common interests. . .and shared goal. . .of prosperity" (Joint Statement on the U.S.-China Strategic and Economic Dialogue Outcomes of the Strategic Track 2012), Varvel's cartoon implicitly questions how committed the United States actually is to the first of these dual objectives.

Political dissidents in China, Rey Chow claims, are hypothetically "traded" in the interest of human rights, becoming fungible products and commoditized tools of a "transnational corporatism" in which humanitarian efforts are subordinated to trading privileges and profit margins (2002:21). Such practices, Chow argues, allow global corporations (and, we might add, their nation states) to avoid the inconvenient "moral embarrassment" of conducting business and political transactions with a communist regime that is perceived to subjugate the human rights of its citizens in the interest of maintaining state power (2002:21). Varvel's cartoon implies, however, that in "Hillary's" sacrifice of Chen, the United States even failed to procure the commodity body. Rather, this appropriation intimates, Chen was abandoned (i.e., pressured to leave the embassy) on the eve of Clinton's opening remarks to the conference, left to face the tanks metaphorically without American support. Here the negotiated body of the Tank Man figure has emerged as a source of economic revenue whose "failed" exchange reflects the currency of a changing world order. Varvel's appropriation of this icon, rather than reinforcing the taken-forgranted meaning of the original, critiques a shifting balance of political power increasingly in favor of China and a waning of the practices and ideologies that in 1989 seemed to embody and guarantee that earlier global hierarchy.

Furthermore, by depicting Chen as reaching out to the "state" in the form of "Hillary" who is absent in the face of war *matériel*, the political cartoonist appears to suggest a gendered inability to act and not simply an unwillingness.13 The state is ineffective, but the cartoon implies in part that this ineffectiveness might be a result of the gender of its presence. Just as this feminization of the state may suggest a lessening of its power, so does calling the secretary of state by her first name, likening one of the United States' most important global representatives to a subordinate figure.14 Thus, the cartoon may be seen as not only lampooning the United States for its diminished authority in the face of Chinese might, but also for its "disempowered" leadership. Although the image does not directly address this, it may be no mistake that this feminization of the state occurs within the context of a late capitalist economic displacement that has witnessed the rapid and powerful rise of China at the expense of the coded masculine factory jobs that have gone to China, where they are often taken over by young women. Given this context, the cartoon could also be read as implying that not only can America no longer be the "provider" of human rights, but neither does it even have the capacity to manufacture the wheelchair in which Chen "protests," doubly helpless in front of the tanks.

The larger global repositioning of nation states suggested in Varvel's cartoon is even more immediately apparent in a second Tank Man appropriation, this one by political cartoonist Dave Brown. Again, the immediate context of the cartoon was a visit to China of a major figure of state, in this case President Barack Obama himself. In addition to the image's direct reference to a reduction of U.S. power in the face of Chinese might, Brown's message about waning state power also appears to contain implicit or coded messages about the domestic arena that are not immediately connected to China. Instead of the gendered implications of the previous cartoon, this one seems to address male economic precarity through a representation of Obama that carries with it unmistakable echoes of earlier caricaturized and racialized images of African Americans.

In this cartoon, published during President Barack Obama's first visit to China in 2009, Brown replaced the figure of the protestor with that of Obama standing in front of a column of Chinese tanks (Brown 2009). In this image of Obama as the Tank Man figure, the artist has replaced the familiar plastic shopping bags with a bucket of soapy water and a dripping wet squeegee. Water puddles at the "hero's" feet. Although Obama's position in the cartoon echoes the defiance of totalitarian threat implied by the original Tank Man image, his disproportionately wide grin appears to be one of trepidation or an attempt to be ingratiating rather than of amusement. Here Obama faces the tanks clad in baggy pants and with his knees bent, apparently signaling submission to state power. Although he offers China his windshield cleaning services, the tanks have no windshields that require his expertise. The United States in this image seems reduced to low-paying service jobs before the might of the Chinese juggernaut, reduced to a lackey of China. Thus, it appears to echo critics of Obama's visit who argued that the president's trip demonstrated "China's ability to push back" and hold firm "against American demands" (Cooper 2009) (Figure 2).

The racial connotations of the Brown image add an extra level of symbolism that reinforces the message about the global repositioning of state power through a caricaturized, racially problematic image that most U.S. viewers will easily recognize as a representation of African American male precarity and race relations in the United States. The cartoon's depiction of Obama as a "squeegee man" refers to a phenomenon that proliferated in New York City in the 1980s and was a target of then-Mayor Rudy Giuliani's famous "Quality of Life" initiative to reduce crime, panhandling, and homelessness throughout the metropolis. The squeegee men were typically unemployed African American men who would approach cars stopped in traffic, perform a cursory cleaning of a motorist's windshields, and then demand payment in what was usually perceived as a threatening manner. In his mayoral campaign, Giuliani vowed to charge the squeegee men with assault if they continued to "menace" and "extort" drivers (Clines 1993).

Although the historically specific structural relations between squeegee-bearing African American males and motorists were not those between states but between ethnicities in which young, emasculated minority men lacked economic empowerment, they directly, mirror the cartoon's wider global scope in which the economic precariousness of the African American man is reformulated as U.S. precariousness in the face of China. On the one hand, we might read this commentary as a critique of race relations in the United States through comparing them with the apparently self-evident disempowerment of the United States relative to China. Yet, in situating Obama in a metonymic relationship to the U.S. state, but one that is occupied by an African American male who is simultaneously disempowered and menacing, the cartoon seems to hint that the effectiveness of state power is ethnically specific. This reinforces hierarchical race relations through posing the president in a servile position reminiscent of earlier racialized iconography that laid bare the "proper" ethnic coding of power.

#### *Domestic Dissent*

Unlike the two images just discussed, which address the relationship between China and the United States, the following two images are motivated by American domestic issues raised during the Occupy Movement of 2011 and seem to locate the threat to freedom and democracy internally, in the form of the abuse of state authority and decline in the power of nonelite citizens. These appropriations suggest that the lack of democratic and human rights that viewers perceived in China in 1989 is now true of the United States in the 21st century. The Occupy Movement, initially a response to government bailouts of major banks and industries during the Great Recession that began in 2009, started with a gathering of protestors in Zuccotti Park in New York City on the tenth anniversary of the post-9/11 reopening of Wall Street. Over the next several months, the movement spread across the nation and the world, with Occupy protests occurring in more than 600 cities and towns in the United States, and 900 cities throughout Europe, Asia, and Africa (Adam 2011). Under the slogan "We are the 99%," the movement reflected popular anger that the wealth of the richest Americans had actually grown during the recession while that of the rest of the population declined considerably, and often precipitously. In response, the movement advocated reinvigorating democracy by promoting greater income and social equality, stopping political and corporate corruption, and reining in laissez-faire financial policies that had led to unprecedented levels of corporate profit as many Americans lost their homes, jobs, and retirement savings.

The movement's largely nonviolent tactics included sit-ins, civil disobedience, Internet activism, strikes, and direct actions that often disrupted or at least discomfited the day-to-day activities of workers and residents of the areas occupied by protestors, many of whom set up tents and other temporary shelters on public land. As the movement gained in strength and duration, however, the responses of officials and police to the protestors became increasingly impatient and even antagonistic, sometimes including clearing protest spaces through force. One of those incidents provided the context for an appropriation of the Tank Man photo that quickly appeared in news stories from San Francisco to Thailand.

In the fall of 2011, a number of students, faculty, and staff in California public universities began to organize marches, rallies, and teach-ins on campuses throughout the state. In mid-November, hundreds of demonstrators gathered at the campus of the University of California, Davis, to protest tuition hikes and state budget cuts, setting up tents and occupying a central campus quadrangle. After several days, university administrators ordered the tents removed, and following the protestors' refusal to leave called in police clad in riot gear to clear demonstrators who blocked pedestrian traffic by sitting on the walkways around the square. Although the activists were acting peacefully, the local police quickly resorted to pepper-spraying the protesting students, several of whom required medical treatment and even hospitalization. Several bystanders recorded the incident on their cell phones and Lieutenant (Lt.) Pike, one of the pepper-spraying police, found himself the object of global derision as the picture of his spewing the noxious pepper spray directly into the faces of peaceful students went immediately viral.16

Media commentators and Internet bloggers instantly began comparing what correspondent James Fallows described as "the shockingly calm brutality of the policeman" to the state violence of Tiananmen Square (Fallows 2011). Among a number of Internet parodies that superimposed the figure of Lt. Pike pepper-spraying innocent bystanders onto a variety of popular images, from a Seurat painting to an advertisement for Tapatio hot sauce, was one that overlaid the image of Lt. Pike onto the original Tank Man photo and quickly spread through the Internet and global media.17 In this new image, Lt. Pike is positioned between the lone protestor and the column of Chinese tanks, merging into the shadow of the gun extending from the tank's turret. This Internet meme unsettles the assumption that the tanks will stop for the lone protestor, for the pith-helmeted, riot gear–clad Pike attempts to remove or incapacitate this obstacle by directing a shocking stream of bright yellow pepper spray into the face of Tank Man. The drama of the original image makes even more shocking the "all-in-a-day's-work" stance of Pike as he ambles slowly by, his casual gait suggesting an everyday stroll in the park (Figure 3).

The situation in this image of Lt. Pike parallels the original in important ways that render the representation recognizable to its viewers. While the scale and the degree of violence of the Davis and Tiananmen protests

In both cases, the protestors won the "moral drama" of the situation (Fallows 2011), even if they ultimately lost their physical battles to the ineffable power of the state. As Allaine Cerwonka discusses in her study of community policing in Australia (2004), even local police reproduce and create power and identity at the level of the nation state through spatial practices that discursively define "moral" national community and hierarchies of social belonging. Lt. Thus, Lt. Pike's actions draw attention to precisely the kinds of disempowerment at the national and global levels addressed in the original Tank Man image and by the Occupy Movement.

The Tank Man image accordingly functions as a visual reference and ideological allusion that offers a historical orientation for contemporary police brutality. As sociologist Craig Calhoun notes of such actions during the Occupy Movement, it is "disturbing to see governments in ostensibly democratic America taking actions reminiscent of the Chinese government" (quoted in Wasserstrom 2011). In this appropriation of the Tank Man image, therefore, the United States no longer occupies the implicit role of an international defender of human rights as it did in the original, but of the adversary of such rights on the domestic front, sacrificing them in the interest of maintaining domestic state power, assaulting freedom of speech and democratic processes with a stream of oleoresin capsicum. This parodic depiction of Lt. Pike as both literally and figuratively in line with the Tiananmen Square tanks is a critical depiction of U.S. state power. While the original iconic photo provided a "graphic shorthand" for the myth of universal aspirations for Western-style democracy (Ghosh 2011:42), this new Tank Man image seems to suggest that achieving and maintaining these aspirations are difficult even in the United States.

#### *Possibilities for Democratic Action*

The last image examined in this article also employs Tank Man imagery to reflect the political vision of the Occupy Movement. In 2011, Zuccotti Park Press commissioned Oakland, CA artist R. Black to design a poster representing an "attack" that featured Wall Street, the five boroughs of New York, and Foley Square.18 When I interviewed Black a year later, he explained to me that Zuccotti Park Press's use of the term *attack* instantly conjured images of military might, which then quickly made him think of the Tank Man image and the courage and bravery it implied for him. Indeed, Black specifically mentioned that he was neither interested in nor concerned with the original Chinese politics of the Tiananmen Square movement, about which he admitted he knew little, but with what the figure of Tank Man implied.

Like the Lt. Pike image, this Occupy poster repurposes the image of the Lone Rebel to comment upon practices of democracy and freedom of speech in the United States, although differing by also suggesting that democratic participation is not limited to individualistic heroism. In what we might describe as coming full circle, Black's appropriation brings back into focus the masses and the voices—the collective nature of the movement—that the ubiquity of the original photograph had erased. Resisting the spectacularization of the lone individual fighting the machinery of the state, the poster offers the collective processes of direct action as the source of a democratic future.

As in the original photo, Black's Tank Man appears standing in front of tanks in the center of the poster, wearing the same black pants and white shirt and carrying plastic bags. Despite these similarities, several major differences reconfigure the layout and meaning of the original image. First, this new Tank Man confronts a horizontal rather than vertical row of three tanks, which are labeled "Wall Street," "Five Boroughs," and "Foley Square." Second, the Tank Man figure is no longer alone, as directly behind him stand countless

other identical Tank Men, joining his effort to halt the tanks collectively in their forward march. In contrast to the original structural simplicity and pale shades of the 1989 photo, the Occupy Tank Man poster is replete with text and bold color. In capital letters that expand in size from left to right, the text above the tanks screams at the viewer to engage in "MASS NON-VIOLENT DIRECT ACTION," "SHUT DOWN WALL STREET," "OCCUPY THE SUBWAYS," and "TAKE THE SQUARE." The directives continue lower in the poster, where smaller text placed in the midst of the anonymous hoards of Tank Men reads "RESIST AUSTERITY," "RECLAIM THE ECONOMY," and "RECREATE OUR DEMOCRACY" (Figure 4).

In this poster, the figure of Tank Man is no longer the only focal point. Rather, the implied cacophony of the combined voices of masses of Tank Men draws the viewer's attention to multiple locations within the image. When viewers are entreated to "RECREATE OUR DEMOCRACY," that democracy is expressed as collective rather than singular. Indeed, there is something both profoundly ironic and appropriate about this remake of the original image. In 1989, the Tank Man protestor was joined by a million-plus activists in Beijing and countless others throughout the nation who also occupied public squares in cities from Nanjing to Chengdu. Likewise, in the poster appropriation, it is the movement, not the lone individual, that confronts the state. Black's poster, therefore, reminds us that the eventfulness of 1989, that transcendent moment of collective consciousness, transcends cultural difference and national boundaries in ways that the iconic Tank Man photograph so successfully elided.19 Thus, Black's poster marks a moment of *commensurability* through drawing upon an icon whose original significance was the production of *incommensurability*.

The anonymity of the Tank Man figure is also important to the meaning of this poster. As noted by human rights activist Robin Munro in PBS's highly viewed documentary on the Tank Man protestor, "He didn't need to have a name. He spoke for the masses, the many who'd been silenced on June 4th. He was all of them. . . . He still doesn't need a name because the point he made, everyone got it [and it] will endure long after this regime has become history" (Frontline 2006). Yet it is not merely that the Tank Man protestor does not *need* to have a name but that to be an effective icon, he *cannot* have a name or a recognizable social or political identity. Indeed, it is this anonymity that enables his enduring presence and permits him to serve as an empty signifier for the projection of Western values and desires. But while the Occupy poster's Tank Man continues to work as an ideological signifier, it is through an alternative form of anonymity that represents power differently. The new message is not so much about individual freedom but about collective liberalism. As Black explained to me, the multiple Tank Man images are intended not as "clones" but to represent a "united force." As such, the poster seems to imply, these multiple Tank Men suggest an alternative to the democratic individualism that the Occupy Movement argues has failed in the U.S. society.

# **Conclusion**

Philosopher Richard Rorty argues that one of the central attributes of modern Western epistemology is its perception of knowledge as a "mirror of nature" (1980). The photograph epitomizes this possibility, offering the viewer a seemingly "truthful" snapshot onto an objective reality. Yet, as John Tagg has noted, photographs are also historical rather than evidence of history (Tagg 1988:247). Thus, when we study Tank Man the protestor as an archetypal figure, we are not merely analyzing a specific event, but an embedded emblem whose meaning is constructed and reconfigured through a particular amalgamation of local historical, social, and political context. These contexts frame regimes of value within which subjects and events signify larger ideological practices and principles. The Tank Man iconic photo performed a set of political relationships that, in the original context, set against a backdrop of *glasnost* in the Soviet Union and democratic reforms in Poland, reinforced Cold War hierarchies of value and provided Western viewers with a taken-forgranted picture of the "natural" order of things. We might argue, as Barthes does in a different context (1972), that the Tank Man icon is a "spectacle of excess," a moment of "total knowledge" that corroborates Western narratives of the nature of good and evil, of human rights, and how democracy works.

This article has explored what happens to such "total knowledge" when icons are appropriated and resituated temporally and geographically, for the nature of the icon—its very ubiquity and obviousness—renders appropriation conspicuous, thus calling into question the semiotic certainties of the original message as they pertain to new historical conditions of experience. When Tank Man—an iconic photo about human rights, heroism, and democratic state–society relations—is lifted from its original context and creatively manipulated, our interpretations are pushed to exceed the firm grip of the ideological coding of the original. What do we learn from these new images about the social and political tensions embodied within its contemporary era and locality? If China, as understood in the West through the image of Tank Man, was the "moral bottom line" (Lee et al. 2011:347), what becomes of the locational certainties of virtue when the United States is perceived to be acting like the imagined Chinese state? The Internet meme, poster, and political cartoons discussed above that appropriate the Tank Man photo's original structure but manipulate its content make ideological inscription present in ways that call into question both the relative positions of China and the United States in the global hierarchy and the "obvious" U.S. embodiment of ideal types of democracy and political practice.

While both forms of the image—the original Tank Man photo and its contemporary appropriations facilitate the self-construction of the West, what makes the images under consideration in this article compelling, even as the original remains an important referential structure, is that their meetings with iconicity tend toward questioning and critique rather than assurance and reproduction. These appropriations do what visual anthropology asks of us, considering the relationship between image and culture, and culture and power (see Pinney 2012:11). They no longer represent a straightforward "comparative anatomy" (Pinney 2012:15) in which China and the United States are ranked on a static evolutionary scale. Rather, they appropriate *and* interrogate: Does the United States emphasize human rights in its international affairs? Do its state–society relations sometimes mirror those of China in 1989? Is individual action the answer to disempowerment? Does capitalism really equate with democracy? The social and political context of the contemporary era, I argue, puts the old Tank Man image into generative conversation with the new Tank Men images found in squeegee anti-heroes and Foley Square to represent power differently and renegotiate political agency, to no longer "see it like a state." These images render evident Michel-Rolph Trouillot's reminder that "not any fiction can pass for history" (1995:29) and as such have the potential to mobilize collective memory and political imaginations to new ends.
