This article explores the significance of the material practices of photography and its archiving in interpretive approaches to the relationship between photographs and history. Drawing on work in material culture studies in anthropology and on the concept of “material hermeneutic,” it argues that photographs should not be understood only through forensic and semiotic analysis of content, but as objects that constitute material performances of a complex range of historiographical desires in the negotiation of the relations among past, present, and future. The analysis is grounded in an exploration of the material practices of the photographic survey movement in England between 1885 and 1918. This loosely cohered group of amateur photographers recorded a historical topography of ancient churches, cottages, passing events, and folk customs in order to create a photographic record for the benefit of future generations. As such it was a self-conscious statement of “popular historicism.” The members’ concern for key values of permanence and accuracy, expressed through the detail of photographic and archival processes, reveals the ways in which cultural loss and photographic loss become mutually sustaining metaphors for each other, and in which the photographs themselves are material markers of both evidential value and of an affective historical imagination. 

_Keywords_ : photography, materiality, evidence, interpretation, archives, historical imagination

These desires cluster around, on the one hand, ideas of truth, accuracy, inscription, and statement, and, on the other, an intense awareness of the past and its potential loss. My heuristic strategy places photographs understood as material things, made in certain ways to fulfill the social and cultural expectations regarding them, at the center of historical analysis. Paying careful attention to the material practices of photography constitutes not only an interpretive strategy, but it also goes to the heart of how the makers of the photographs themselves saw the potential of their images. Indeed doubly so: the material practices of photography were not only performances by the photographers with their particular aspirations regarding the past, but the subjects of these photographs were themselves physical or material traces of the historical past. understanding the saliency of these entwined practices for those involved is a way of exploring the potential of photographs as historical evidence, and is also a means to investigate the forms through which historical imagination might be made possible and be experienced. 

I developed this approach in doing a historical-anthropological study of the photographic survey movement in England between 1885–1918. This loosely cohered group attempted to harness the energies of amateur photographers from a broad social range—hobby photographers, members of local camera clubs, and the photographic sections of natural history, archaeological, and antiquarian societies (and between 1897 and 1910, the national Photographic record Association)—to produce a visual record of subjects of “historical interest” for the benefit of future generations. The result was a historical topography manifested through antiquities, built environment, folk customs, current events of historical interest, and, in the more ambitious, geology and natural history. These photographic surveys were part of that broader cultural matrix of preservation, recording, and archiving in the late nineteenth century, which embraced a wide range of cultural productions, from architecture and folklore to footpaths and dialect speech. however, what makes the photographic surveys particularly interesting in this context are their self-conscious declarations about the relationship between photography and historical value, and the way in which material practices are at the center of their production of this value. This amateur expression of photographic enthusiasm and historical desire is thus a useful prism through which to explore our own historical approaches to photographs. 

The result of these photographic survey efforts, often referred to by contemporaries as “history photos,” was a series of local archives, available in local free libraries open to all, that reproduced these sets of values and expressed and consolidated a sense of local historical depth in the popular imagination. Through these activities photographers effectively defined who they were and what they perceived as valuable knowledge for future benefit. In other words, the photographs, as a “popular historicism,”<sup>2</sup> were intended to preserve and perform a perception of the historical past, which would inspire a cohesive social good, which

Through these complex and ambiguous manifestations of what I term “historical desire,” the survey movement anticipated the dynamic potential of the historical in the future, as time, including future time, was translated into the space of the archive. 

Although the original results of these endeavors were perhaps less extensive and more uneven than enthusiasts for photographic survey and “history pictures” might have liked, the scale of the movement’s ambition and vision has to be recognized as integral to the discourse. yet, over the years, these ambitions have been dissipated, resulting in a confused, fragmented, and dispersed archive that not only tells us much about shifting evaluations of photographs as historical evidence and its institutionalization, but significantly here, presents us with methodological problems. My research has revealed some seventy-five surveys based in English counties, cities, and towns (and a few in scotland, Ireland, and Wales). There were probably more that have eluded me to date, especially the short-lived failures. Although largely independent, all aligned themselves self-consciously to common objectives and sets of values, articulated largely in the national and local press, on the historical potential of record photography to provide an archive for the benefit of future generations. of these seventy-five I have examined sixteen in detail,<sup>11</sup> yielding an image dataset of about 55,000 prints, about 1,000 active amateur photographers, and some hundreds of supporters and facilitators. simple arithmetic points to the possible number of photographs that were made under the rubric of “survey photography,” and the number of people involved, even if we have lost archival sight of many of them. 

The methodological question must therefore be, how can we think about, and think with, this morass of material? Methodologies concerned with critical forensic and semiotic readings tend to have their roots in either a traditional art-historical address of single images or in psychology and visual communication. With such a mass of disparate images—of churches, cottages, manor houses, whipping posts, ancient trackways, shepherd’s smocks, types of cattle, beehives, cathedrals, castles, folk customs, rood screens, dog-gates, funeral monuments, city streets, children’s games—clearly the analysis of single images is not only impracticable, but will obscure as much as it reveals. franco Moretti has asked, in his study of nineteenth-century novels, how do we understand the dispersed fields beyond the canon? he argues that studies of the nineteenth-century novel have been premised on about 200 works representing about one percent of the novels produced during the period, to the exclusion of the rest.<sup>12</sup> The same goes for photographs, despite the expanding base of research. how do we account for the massive and bewildering array of photographs such as I have just described? heterogeneity is

### II. PosITIonIngs 

As a result, I am going to bring to the surface the ways in which the moral values, historical desires, and social expectations of the survey movement that clustered around ideas of photographic evidence, realism, permanence, aesthetics, and technique were articulated through the debates around the material production, organizing, archiving, and dissemination of its photographs (what christopher Pinney has recently described as their “techno-materiality”<sup>14</sup> ). I shall argue that this materiality constituted the very fabric of the idea of photographic evidence, as the physical production of photographs was made especially to carry historical and moral weight. 

A formative strand of my approach is work in visual and material anthropology, which is concerned with the way in which social relations, cultural values, and the materiality of things are mutually constituted. As Daniel Miller and christopher Tilley put it: “an adequate understanding of any social actions and relations [including historical ones] . . . demands an understanding of material culture, and vice versa.”<sup>15</sup> In recent years there has been gathering analytical interest in the socio-material practices of photography. While the ground was perhaps laid in the 1960s by Pierre Bourdieu’s analysis of bourgeois photographic practices,<sup>16</sup> this line of thinking has more recently been reformulated and reinvigorated by the application of ideas developed in material culture studies and in the anthropology of photography.<sup>17</sup> In this approach, which stresses the agency of objects in human relations, “things” such as photographs emerge as powerful and active players, extending or replacing embodied experiences and connecting with spiritual ones, such as, in the present case, historical experience, imagination, and memory.

Miller has argued that rather than thinking only about how things signify, which he maintains privileges an intellectualized response to objects, we should ask, in a way that resonates with ideas of historical desire, “how things matter.” “‘Mattering’ has,” he argues, “a more diffused, almost sentimental association that is more likely to lead us to the concerns of those being studied than those doing the studying,” and is thus a way of allowing a space for the subjective.<sup>18</sup> as he puts it, “[B]y dwelling upon the more mundane sensual and material qualities of the object [here a glass plate, a platinum print, or a label] we are able to unpick the more subtle connections with cultural lives and values that are objectified through these forms, in part, because of the particular qualities they possess.”<sup>19</sup> 

Thus much is to be gained analytically through understanding the specific ways in which different material forms both become meaningful and produce meaning themselves as they emerge from dense intersections of value and materiality. thinking about photographs in this way reveals them to be not merely symbols of the past, a generic form of pastness, but material forms that arise out of the intersection of experiences and imaginings of the past. This “active materiality” is no better demonstrated than in the discourse of the survey movement in which the accuracy, truthfulness, and authority of the socially active historical statement was technically and materially performed through the attention given to the exact nature of objects, be they photographic prints or a box of photographs in the archive. 

significantly, there are parallel resonances with this approach in recent writing on historiographical theory, sometimes drawing on the same body of literature, notably Bruno Latour’s writing on the networks of relations between humans and non-humans. The survey movement was shaped by a striking unity and consistency of discourse, which was dominated by two key values that defined its hopes for photographs: accuracy and permanence. These values were, as I have already mentioned, articulated through morally inflected material objects—photographic negatives, contact prints, enlargements, labels, mounts, boxes, and albums. It was a discourse that focused attention on marks on surfaces—of chemicals on paper, ink on labels, or even the potential danger of thumbprints on mounts—which, as Peter Maynard has argued, constitute the nature of photography and responses to it.<sup>26</sup> these practices were not simply an attempt to homogenize both production and the archive, but rather to achieve a procedural correctness that would perform the values of empirical reliability, legibility, and validity through attention to the regularity of material practices, therefore safeguarding against the loss, either chemically or contextually, of any element of the historical object and thus of history itself. In the survey movement there was a self-conscious engagement with the nature of photographic inscription. The rendering of the object in front of the camera was to be defined by moral values of the straightforward and “ordinary” and by self-restraint in the embrace of the collective good over individual vision.<sup>27</sup>

This conscious engagement with the mechanical tracing of photography, control of subjectivity, and, importantly here, the precise physical forms photographs should take, might be seen as a concern with “truth to sources.” This concept, of course, permeated the developing historiographical practices of the nineteenth century, stressing systematic approaches to original sources and the sense of the voice of the manuscript. It was in survey photography that one might find the visual equivalent of these values,<sup>28</sup> as photographers attempted to grasp and articulate the “truth to sources” through the accurate, “straightforward” transcription and rendering of a parish church or medieval market cross. 

But values other than accuracy also concerned the survey photographers. The tensions of historical desire, between the loss of the past and the dynamic possibility of the future, saturate its practices. There was a concern with the loss of the potential of photographs to provide historical evidence that might be described as “entropic anxiety.” It is this that might be understood as the cohering metaphor of the survey movement. This permeates all levels of the movement, and their material practices can be seen as a battle against forces that were relentlessly and increasingly perceived as random and disordered: against the frailty of human memory, against the forces of disordered modernity, and against cultural and material disappearance. only through the containment of such threats could the dynamic future be achieved and maintained. 

such concerns were part of a larger culture of entropic anxiety. Thomas richards has argued that during the latter part of the nineteenth century, notably in its imperial perspective, concepts of “information loss” and the measure of disorganization had become more forceful with advances in theoretical physics, notably the second Law of Thermodynamics, which was concerned with the distribution and loss or dissipation of heat, energy, and power. As both stephen Brush and greg Myers have demonstrated in their different ways, not only were these ideas inflected with social theory, but they were absorbed into the public imagination as the language of physics came to be used over a number of fields including social and moral criticism and history itself.<sup>29</sup> Applied to mathematics, statistics, and then the macroeconomics of the waste and conservation of capital, the concept and device of “entropy” which emerged from this became, as richards describes it, “the important epistemological index of the century.”<sup>30</sup> by the late nineteenth century it had become, among other things, a theory of information management of metaphorical force, as society struggled to cope with the possibility of too much knowledge, which might explode and dissipate at any moment.

The practices of enlarging presented another site of material instability. I have argued here for a “material hermeneutic” to enrich our understanding of the production of historical evidence, for it was a production saturated in concerns for the material presence of that evidence and the material forms of its translation. I would argue that, in methodological terms, it is only by employing a “material hermeneutic,” paying attention to things and practices, and moving the analysis of photographs from questions of representation alone to questions of material practice, that we can actually comprehend the role of photography in the discipline of history. This role consists not only in producing historical evidence in a forensic sense, but also in expressing a self-conscious historiographical desire to inscribe, narrate, and connect to the past. for the properties of materials—the softness of platinum prints, the fragility of surfaces, and the marks on cards—are not mere attributes, but themselves are constituted through the social relations, values, and emotional investments that they embody. The potential of a material approach lies not merely in method but in the acknowledgment of the nature of culture itself and the worlds of practice it creates.<sup>72</sup> It is precisely such close attention to the way the material performs the intersection of historical and photographic desire that forces a more profound engagement with the nature of the historical statement so produced.
